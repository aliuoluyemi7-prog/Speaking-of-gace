What Is Grace?

By Salt Oluyemi · 9 min read

There is a Yoruba proverb my grandmother used to say: "Ajeji ọwọ́ kan kò gbé ẹrù dórí." A lone hand cannot lift a heavy load onto the head. It takes another hand, another back, another set of shoulders. You do not carry the heaviest things alone.

I have come to believe that grace is God saying the same thing about your soul.

The Word We Use Too Easily

We say "grace" so often in church that it has become furniture — something in the room we no longer really see. We say grace before meals. We name our daughters Grace. We sing about amazing grace without pausing long enough to ask what, exactly, is so amazing about it.

So let us slow down and ask the question honestly: what is grace?

Paul answers it plainly in his letter to the Ephesians:

"For by grace you have been saved through faith. And this is not your own doing; it is the gift of God, not a result of works, so that no one may boast." (Ephesians 2:8–9)

Grace is God's unearned favor toward people who did nothing to deserve it and could do nothing to earn it. Not a discount on a price you still had to pay. Not a head start you still had to finish. A gift, completely given, before you had anything to offer in return.

That last phrase — so that no one may boast — is not a throwaway line. It is the whole point. Grace is designed, on purpose, to remove your ability to take credit. If you could have earned your way to God, you would spend forever quietly reminding Him of it. Grace closes that door before you can even reach for the handle.

Why This Is Hard to Receive

Here is what I have learned after twenty years of learning and relearning, and years wrestling with my own heart: human beings are far more comfortable earning than receiving.

Earning feels safe because it feels controllable. If I perform well enough, pray hard enough, sin little enough, I can predict the outcome. I am in charge of my own outcome. Receiving, on the other hand, requires trust — it means someone else is in charge, and I simply open my hands.

This is why Paul spends so much of Romans dismantling every human strategy for self-rescue before he ever gets to grace:

"None is righteous, no, not one... for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." (Romans 3:10, 23)

Notice the order. Before grace can be understood as a gift, the diagnosis has to be understood as serious. If sin were a minor stain, we would want soap, not salvation. But Scripture describes something closer to spiritual death — not a person who needs improvement, but a person who needs to be raised. Grace is only "amazing" once you have honestly faced what it was rescuing you from.

Grace Revealed at the Cross

Grace is not an abstract concept. It has a shape, and the shape is a cross.

"But God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us." (Romans 5:8)

Notice again: while we were still sinners. Not after we cleaned up. Not once we qualified. Christ did not wait at the finish line for us to arrive — He met us at the starting line, while we were still running the wrong direction entirely.

Jesus told a story about this that I return to again and again — the parable of the prodigal son (Luke 15:11–32). Everyone remembers the younger son who wastes his inheritance and comes home in disgrace. Fewer people notice that the story has two lost sons. The younger brother is lost through obvious rebellion. The older brother — the one who stayed home, did everything right, and never left — is lost through something quieter: the belief that his obedience had earned him a standing his father owed him.

Both sons needed grace. One needed to be forgiven. The other needed to realize he was never working for wages in the first place — he was already a son.

That is the scandal in the title of the course growing out of this platform: grace is scandalous because it offends both the people who think they have failed too badly to receive it, and the people who think they have succeeded too well to need it.

Grace Is Not Permission — It Is Power

I want to address something honestly, because I have watched this misunderstanding do real damage: grace is not a license to stay the same.

"For the grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation for all people, training us to renounce ungodliness and worldly passions, and to live self-controlled, upright, and godly lives in the present age." (Titus 2:11–12)

Read that carefully. Grace does not merely forgive us and leave us where we were. Grace trains us. The same grace that removes our guilt also reshapes our appetites. This is the difference between a legal pardon and a living transformation — grace gives you both, and neither one is optional or partial.

This is also why grace and legalism are not opposites in the way people assume. Legalism says: obey, and then you will belong. License says: you belong, so obedience doesn't matter. Grace says something neither of them can say: you already belong — and that belonging is precisely what makes a new kind of obedience possible. Not obedience to earn love, but obedience that flows from already being loved.

The Community Grace Creates

Grace does not stop at the individual. Scripture is clear that what we have received, we are meant to extend:

"Put on then, as God's chosen ones, holy and beloved, compassionate hearts, kindness, humility, meekness, and patience, bearing with one another and, if one has a complaint against another, forgiving each other; as the Lord has forgiven you, so you also must forgive." (Colossians 3:12–13)

A person who has truly understood grace becomes a different kind of neighbor, a different kind of parent, a different kind of colleague — because grace given is grace that wants to be passed on. This is why "Speaking of Grace" is not just a name for theology. It is meant to describe a way of speaking to and about other people.

Why This Matters for Everything Else Here

If you read nothing else on this platform, I want you to have understood this: grace is God's free, undeserved gift of righteousness and relationship, given through Christ, received by faith alone, and powerful enough to actually change the person who receives it.

Every article on Grace and Gospel, every study in Theology, every reflection on Christian living, every course inside the Fellowship — all of it is an attempt to keep saying this one true thing in as many honest ways as I can find, for as long as I'm given breath to write.

Ajeji ọwọ́ kan kò gbé ẹrù dórí. You were never meant to lift this alone. That is the whole of the Gospel, and it is the whole of what "grace" means.

Grace in One Minute

  • Grace is God's undeserved gift, not a wage you earned.
  • Grace is received by faith, not achieved by works.
  • Grace both forgives you and transforms you — it is pardon and power together.
  • Grace removes boasting and grows humility, because there is nothing left to take credit for.
  • Grace changes how you relate to God, and it changes how you treat everyone else.
